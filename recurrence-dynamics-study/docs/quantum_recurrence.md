# Quantum recurrence

## State-space distinction

A finite-dimensional Hilbert space is not a finite set of states. Normalized
vectors form a continuum, and physical pure states form a continuous
projective space after global phase is removed.

## Exact unitary recurrence

For a time-independent Hamiltonian,

\[
\lvert\psi(t)\rangle
 = \sum_n c_n e^{-iE_nt/\hbar}\lvert E_n\rangle .
\]

Physical exact recurrence at time \(T\) means

\[
\lvert\psi(T)\rangle=e^{i\phi}\lvert\psi(0)\rangle .
\]

For each pair of occupied energy levels,

\[
\frac{(E_n-E_m)T}{\hbar}\in 2\pi\mathbb Z .
\]

Exact recurrence therefore requires commensurate relevant energy gaps.
Generic incommensurate gaps do not produce an exact positive return.

## Near recurrence

When only finitely many energy levels are occupied, the relative phases move
on a finite-dimensional torus. Simultaneous Diophantine approximation yields
arbitrarily close returns. Infinite-dimensional systems require additional
spectral and state assumptions, and a continuous spectral component may
invalidate the finite-torus argument.

## Physical limits

- Equality is defined up to global phase; relative phase remains observable.
- Unitary recurrence applies to a closed state, not generally to a decohering
  subsystem.
- Finite entropy or effective finite dimension does not imply a finite number
  of exact quantum states.
- Quantum recurrence does not establish cosmological periodicity.
